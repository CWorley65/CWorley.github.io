Take the time to amble to the buffet...

I was in a play during my Junior year. I may not remember the name of the play, but I do slightly remember the part. I played the prototypical "nerd" in some high school, whatever that really means. At one point during the play was about high, I stood there talking to one of my classmates describing the lunch scence at it just struck me as odd. I described that you have your jocks in one corner, your aggies in another corner, your nerds over here, this group over there, and everyone else just slammed in the middle of the cafeteria. It really made me think. What group did I belong to? Can I belong to more than one group? The answer was and is a resounding YES.

I am and always have belonged to what seemed like multiple cliques. For starters, I have always been what most classify as a nerd. I enjoyed school. If it were up to me, I'd stay in school for as long as possible. But, my mother once told me that I wasn't allowed to be the "Librarian" (a TNT movie that morphed into a sequels and even a series). Staying on the school front, I played as many sports as I could. I played football through high school and college. I tried my hand miserably at basketball. I "ran" track as well though most of the time I stuck to the field events. There was a year where I played baseball. I embraced the co-curricular activities. As soon as I was old enough, I even joined the volunteer fire department in my hometown as a first responder and firefighter.

Lastly, but most importantly, I am an aggie. I grew up on one of the largest family-owned farms in the Commonwealth of Virginia. Our farm has been in continuous operations for well over a century. Since the late 1980s, my family has raised vegetables that have been in all matter of stores not only in Virginia, but also all over the East Coast. The older I got, the longer the days seemed to get. At the time, it seemed like it was way too much work, but now I appreciate it for what it gave me: my work ethic. It gave me the ability to reach a solution to most problems that I come across.

So how does an offensive lineman, firefighting farmer end up with a PhD in mathematics of all things?

Well, the answer is loaded. Like I said earlier, I loved school. I enjoyed being right, and hated being wrong. (Something that I eventually had to overcome. Math definitely teaches me that.) Over the course of my education, I saw many paths for my future. Each one was the one before it wasn't. Funny how that works out sometimes. I can remember in the second grade that I wanted to be an ornithologist (and yes I knew that word back then). At one point, I wanted to be a weatherman. Then, when I realized that I really was good with numbers and math, I settled on being an engineer (I just couldn't decide on any one specialty). There's a story that my grandfather loved to tell about me in the fourth grade that involves me calculating the bill for a woman buying some vegetables where I raced her and her calculator. I may have to tell you all some time.

Then some profound words were brought to my attention in strange way. A former teacher wrote in a yearbook from high school of mine. He said that I should take the time to amble over to the buffet. I remembered these words about the time that I started taking some great math courses in college. My advisor, who happened to be teaching the course at the time, suggested that I switch from engineering to math. I resisted for awhile until that piece of advice came back to me. I knew that I enjoyed the challenge that math posed. I saw the beauty and interconnectedness of the courses. I started seeing a bigger picture the further I went, and then I was hooked. The rest as they say is history at least up to now.

    An Introduction to 3D Printing

    As a direct application of geometric and analytical thinking this course would teach students computer-aided design (CAD) through applications like Tinkercad and Fusion 360. Students will apply geometric reasoning to design, draft, 3D print, and iterate models to solve real life problems. Students will learn about the process of Fused Deposition Modeling and Stereolithgraphy in this course. The course also includes an introduction to the hardware, firmware, and software involved in 3D printing along with how mathematics is applied in these.     Calculus I

    From MC's 2020-21 catalog: An introduction to calculus using computer technology. Topics include functions, limits, the derivative and its applications, and the definite integral. All topics are presented geometrically, numerically, and algebraically.

    I have taught this class at both the University of Tennessee, Knoxville, and at Maryville College in both a regular semester and during the summer term. Most recently during the Summer of 2020, as an online course using MyOpenMath as the homework system.

    Calculus I at Maryville College is part of our Core Curriculum in the Mathematical Reasoning domain.

    Calculus II

    A continuation of Calculus I. Topics include antiderivatives, Riemann Sums, Integration techniques, sequences, series, and power series.

    I have taught this course at both UTK and MC.

    Calculus III

    A continuation of Calculus II. From MC's 2020-21 catalog, Calculus III is a course in multivariable calculus using computer technology. Topics include functions of several variables, vectors, partial differentiation, multiple integration, parametric equations, vector fields, and line integrals.

    I have taught this course at both UTK and MC. At UTK, I taught Calculus III as a flipped class using Mathematica for various calculations. I put most of the focus on students' ability to explain WHY they used the process they used in order to reach the numerical answer that they did.

    Precalulus with Trigonometry

    A traditional continuation of college algebra. We studied function notation, real and complex number systems, polynomial, rational, exponential, logarithmic, and trigonometric functions, their properties, and their applications.

    Quantitative Literacy

    Quant Lit serves as one of the two Core domains at Maryville College. Ever student must take this course as a requirement for graduation. Topics include percentages, fractions, ratios and proportions, linear and exponential modeling, intro probability, descriptive statistics, financial mathematics, and voting methods among other topics through a problem-solving approach. This course is designed for students to interpret quantitaive data, make arguments, and draw appropriate conclusions. We also have multiple projects that promote teamwork and self-growth.

    Currently, we are using David Lippman's "Math in Society" as our main textbook along with MyOpenMath as our homework platform. One of our goals in this course has been to reduce the cost for students by promoting open sourced materials.

    Discrete Structures

    Topics included logic and proofs, recurrence relations, functions, sets, grpahs, an introduction to formal languages, induction, and other topics. This course served as the bridge course for both computer science and mathematics students as they moved towards their upper level courses. A fair amount of time was spent on the students' mathematical writing skills and learning to typeset using LaTeX.
